# 2026-07-18 to 2026-07-19 — What we achieved and learned

A working log, not a polished piece — raw material for later storytelling, kept honest about
what actually happened rather than smoothed into a narrative.

Cross-cutting, project-level-milestone stretch: closed out two long-running investigations
(vocabulary-discrepancy, Role Meaning), reassessed and decided the roadmap around a genuinely new
distinction (Backlog Evolution vs. Story Readiness), then executed two of its three iterations for
real — including one that surfaced a live gap neither existing mechanism could have caught.

---

## Context

The prior stretch had left the vocabulary-discrepancy check mid-validation (mechanism confirmed,
signal partially confirmed, human validation still open). This stretch ran that validation for
real — first this agent acting as a careful but non-independent reviewer, then a genuinely
independent human session — and the real data forced a harder correction than expected: the check
wasn't just imprecise, it was operating at the wrong abstraction level entirely (words, not
concepts). That correction cascaded into retiring two investigation tracks, reframing "backlog
discovery" around concepts already known to Canopy, deciding a concrete 3-iteration execution plan,
and running two of those iterations against the real dogfooding project — one of which produced the
sharpest evidence yet that "this story is internally complete" and "this capability area is safe to
build" are genuinely different claims.

---

## Shipped

### Vocabulary-discrepancy check: mechanism validated, signal validated, then retired
Four unscripted sessions (this agent as reviewer) showed the signal is a real, classifiable mix —
not proof it generalizes to independent humans. A fifth, genuinely independent session (the project
owner's own real dogfooding work) landed at 95% not-meaningful / 5% meaningful / 0% not-sure —
sharply more skewed than this agent's own distribution, and revealing something more specific than
imprecision: the one confirmed hit (`catalog`) named a real concept; nearly everything dismissed
was either a verb/adjective, or — the sharpest cases — a word that was already covered by an
existing compound entity name (`variant` vs. already-registered `ProductVariant`) or an existing
synonym relationship with zero shared characters (`sale` vs. already-seeded `Order`). Word-level
matching structurally cannot catch either failure shape. Retired as an investigation, kept as
shipped code.

### Role Meaning and its supporting tracks: retired
Already concluded before this stretch (`role-meaning-collapses-to-classification.md`); formally
closed as an active track in the roadmap update below, alongside the Domain Exploration MVP vision
and the operational-fact collection-strategy genre of work that produced it.

### Roadmap reassessment → decided backlog execution plan
Reframed the entire recent thread around a new distinction: **Backlog Evolution** (does a known
concept have a story yet) vs. **Story Readiness** (is this already-existing story internally
sound) — named as separate concerns being conflated by Stage 0's own narrow scope. Entity-with-
no-story (comparing `domain_registry.yaml` against `stories.yaml`'s `as_a`/`want` fields) was
identified as the strongest concept-level signal: cheapest to build, zero new data needed, and
already showing 8 concrete uncaptured entities (`Customer`, `Order`, `Cart`, `Payment`, `Supplier`,
`Review`, `Discount`, `Notification`) in the real project. Decided, in writing
(`docs/design/roadmap-reassessment.md`'s 2026-07-18 update), as three ordered iterations —
entity-with-no-story, a Human-Insight Inventory rerun, and a `Customer` vertical slice — rather than
left as further analysis.

### Iteration 1: entity-with-no-story, shipped and live-verified
Mechanical check wired into `canopy domain` (a whole-project, on-demand, non-blocking view) rather
than embedded in `intent`'s per-story flow — a deliberate placement correction from where the
retired word-level check lived. Live-verified against the real project: caught a real bug
immediately (an all-caps acronym, `SKU`, mangled by the PascalCase-splitting logic into `"s k u"`,
wrongly flagging an already-covered entity) — fixed same session, regression-tested, reinstalled,
reconfirmed. Final result: 11 genuinely uncovered entities.

### A dependency evaluation selected `Customer` as the first vertical slice
Mapped all 11 candidates by prerequisite chain, effort, unlock potential, and destination-vs-
supporting shape. `Customer` was the only one with zero unsatisfied prerequisites, the highest
unlock count (five other entities depend on it existing first), and destination-capability status
— the literal next link in the manufacturer→model→product→variant→catalog chain this project has
been building since `product-001`.

### Iteration 3: the `Customer` vertical slice, real, through contract generation
`product-010` ("browse the published catalog") carried through `intent → spec → behaviors →
contracts` for real. Along the way: a real, live reproduction of an already-known pipeline defect
(Stage 1 returned zero behaviors for all 5 scenarios on the first attempt, despite a verified-
correct prompt — the same failure class the Human Insight Process Experiment already documented);
declined to proceed past it, checked the log, retried, and the retry succeeded cleanly.

**The more important result**: the finished story passed every existing readiness check — Stage 0
(`gaps: []`), Stage 2 (`decisions: []`), Stage 3 and Stage 4 (both `findings: []`) — while its own
artifacts directly contradicted each other. `out_of_scope` explicitly excluded "Customer
authentication and authorization"; the accepted scenarios and contract included a full
401-Unauthorized behavior; no authentication capability exists anywhere in the project. Traced
precisely why each stage missed it (not vaguely — Stage 0 never cross-reads `out_of_scope` against
scenario content; Stage 2 only processes items already flagged `unresolved`, and this one was
misfiled as `resolved` with an absence cited as if it were a fact; Stage 3/4 check structural, not
semantic, soundness) and recorded it as an open question rather than jumping to a fix.

### Two existing principles' own predicted-but-untested gaps, confirmed live
Both `unresolved-decisions-become-explicit-decision-points.md` and `cross-artifact-consistency-
audits-prevent-drift.md` had named, in their own "Future Validation" sections, the exact failure
shapes `product-010` produced — a citation that doesn't verify its claim, and a semantic (not
lexical) consistency check that's never been tried. Extended both principles' evidence lists with
this real instance; confidence ratings unchanged (already `high`; this confirms a named risk, it
doesn't newly discover one).

## Learned

- **Abstraction level matters more than precision.** A detector operating on the wrong unit
  (words) can't be fixed by getting better at that unit — it needs replacing with one operating on
  the right unit (concepts). This took a genuinely independent human session to surface; this
  agent's own careful reasoning, applied to the same words, didn't reveal the abstraction mismatch
  on its own.
- **"Internally complete" and "safe to build" are different claims, now with a concrete instance,
  not just an assertion.** Every stage the pipeline currently runs reported clean on `product-010`.
  The gap wasn't a bug in any one stage — it was that no stage was ever scoped to check the
  specific thing that broke (a same-story field contradiction, grounded against project-wide
  capability state).
- **Known pipeline defects are cheaper to work around than to re-litigate.** The Stage 1
  zero-behaviors failure reproduced exactly as documented weeks earlier; the right response was
  the same as before (check the log, confirm the prompt was correct, retry) — not a fresh
  investigation into a already-understood compliance limitation.
- **A principle's own "Future Validation" section is a standing prediction, worth checking against
  new evidence as it arrives** — both principles extended here had already, in writing, named the
  exact gap `product-010` exposed, before it happened. Confirming a named prediction is real
  evidence capture; it doesn't need a new principle to hold it.

## Iteration 2 — Human-Insight Inventory Rerun (2026-07-19)

Reran the original inventory's counting method, unchanged, against every real review-gate outcome
accumulated since (`docs/design/human-insight-inventory-rerun.md`). Sample grew from one story to
two (`manufacturer-001`, `product-010`), plus nine intent-stage sessions and 21 genuine
project-owner vocabulary-discrepancy judgments (Run #3 of the now-retired vocabulary check). A
provenance split mattered here the same way it did in the original pass: Run #1/#2 of the
vocabulary check (scripted defaults / this agent's own classification) were excluded from every
count as not genuine human signal, matching the same "anchor first" discipline CLAUDE.md's
Diagnosing Dogfooding Runs section prescribes.

**Result: the original finding sharpens rather than changes.** Across 42 real review actions,
exactly one deviated from uniform Accept — the project's very first intent-stage proposal, rejected
for a mechanically obvious defect (wrong actor, `want` field not naming the entity) — before this
inventory's tracked categories even existed. Every subsequent domain-judgment-shaped
recommendation (service ownership, tech stack, infrastructure, domain-event determination,
vocabulary discrepancy) was Accepted without a single Modify or Reject, across two stories, two
domains. The Decision Point mechanism (Stage 2) has now been confirmed, twice, to have never fired
at all in real use — zero opportunities to modify or reject, not zero modifications.

**"Too early to tell" splits into two questions with different answers, not one that resolves
uniformly** — the rerun deliberately kept them apart rather than reporting a single verdict:
- Does the review gate differentiate scrutiny by category among proposals with real data? **Yes,
  now answerable**: no, it doesn't — confirmed pattern, not single-story anecdote.
- Should domain-event determination become a Decision Point? **Still genuinely too early to
  tell** — not from thin evidence, but because the category's sample size didn't move (still n=1;
  the one new story to reach `spec` never produced a domain-event proposal at all).
